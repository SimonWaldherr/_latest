<p>The French government said Tuesday it would bring social media giants "under control," following the role that online platforms played in the beheading of a history teacher in Paris on Friday.</p><p>Moments after Samuel Paty, 47, was killed, a picture of his head was posted on Twitter. Before his death, videos of him showing caricatures of the Prophet Muhammad in a class about free speech were circling on social media.</p><p>He was killed in what President Emmanuel Macron has called the killing an "<a href="https://www.businessinsider.com/teacher-beheaded-in-paris-after-showing-cartoons-of-prophet-muhammad-2020-10">Islamist terrorist attack</a>."</p><p>"Things began on social media and they ended on social media," Gabriel Attal, the French government spokesman, said Tuesday. "We have to do better at bringing them under control."</p>

The Paris police later shot dead the suspected attacker &#8212; identified by local media as Abdoulakh Anzorov, an 18-year-old Chechen refugee &#8212; near the scene of the attack.&#160;</p><p>Paty, a 47-year-old teacher, had <a href="https://www.businessinsider.com/teacher-beheaded-in-paris-after-showing-cartoons-of-prophet-muhammad-2020-10">shown his pupil's caricatures of the Prophet Muhammad</a> from the satirical newspaper Charlie Hebdo as part of an obligatory "moral and civil education" course before he was decapitated.</p>
