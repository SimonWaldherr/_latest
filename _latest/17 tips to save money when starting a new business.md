                                    <p>One of the easiest ways to prevent excess spending when starting a business is by making informed tax decisions. The first step is determining where in the US you're starting the company &#8212; different states have various tax rules for startups.</p><p>Founders must also choose how they want to structure the business. That factor dictates the income tax return a business taxpayer must file. The most common structures are sole proprietorship, partnership, corporation, S corporation, and a limited liability company, <a href="https://www.irs.gov/newsroom/six-tips-for-people-starting-a-new-business">according to the IRS</a>.&#160;</p><p>The next step is picking a tax year, which is the annual accounting period for reporting income, expenses, and keeping records. There are two options: A calendar year, which runs from January 1 to December 31, and a fiscal year, which ends on the last day of any month except for December.&#160;</p><p>Paperwork is also vital. Entrepreneurs should apply for an employer identification number (also known as an EIN) &#8212; most businesses need an EIN, according to the IRS. Meanwhile, founders should ensure that the company's mailing address, location, and responsible party are up to date.</p><p>They should also have employees fill out important documents like form I-9 &#8212; which identifies an employee's identity and work authorization &#8212; and the employee's withholding certificate W-4.&#160;</p><p>Lastly, don't forget to pay your taxes. There are 5 general types of business taxes that entrepreneurs must pay, including income tax, estimated taxes, self-employment tax, employment tax, and excise tax. To determine what you owe, founders must look at their employees, business structure, and goods or services. Consult a tax specialist or the <a href="https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/business-taxes">IRS website if you're unsure</a>.&#160;</p>
