<div><p class="css-158dogj evys1bk0">&#8220;Summer is over, and the fun is over,&#8221; Ms. McKay said.</p><p class="css-158dogj evys1bk0">In several interviews in the past several weeks, city residents shared deep frustrations with their fellow New Yorkers who don&#8217;t appear to be following the same rules. The solidarity forged in the springtime outbreak appears, in some neighborhoods, to have fractured in the fall.</p><p></p><p class="css-158dogj evys1bk0">Michael Mitchell, a 49-year-old therapist from Washington Heights, said he was dismayed by what he saw on a recent subway ride. &#8220;People were not wearing masks &#8212; I asked them to put them on, and they wouldn&#8217;t,&#8221; he said. &#8220;So I feel like that sense of community living that we had is broken.&#8221;</p><p class="css-158dogj evys1bk0">Andre Williams, 45, the father who has resumed stockpiling acetaminophen and other supplies for his family in Clinton Hill, put it bluntly: &#8220;A lot of us took our eyes off the ball.&#8221;</p><p class="css-158dogj evys1bk0">Jennifer Burchette, a 29-year-old publicist from Bedford-Stuyvesant who changed her plans to return to her office, said too many people are acting as if the threat has passed.</p><p class="css-158dogj evys1bk0">&#8220;It&#8217;s not over because you&#8217;re over it,&#8221; she said.</p><p class="css-158dogj evys1bk0">In a survey of 1,000 New York City residents conducted in late September, 72 percent of the respondents said they expected a second wave of cases that will resemble the surge of April, according to the <a class="css-1g7m0tk" href="https://sph.cuny.edu/research/covid-19-tracking-survey/" title="" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">City University of New York Graduate School of Public Health and Health Policy</a>, which released the findings on Wednesday. But beneath that fear were signs of optimism.</p><p></p><p class="css-158dogj evys1bk0">Seventy percent of the respondents said they planned to stay in the city for the duration of its recovery. And the number of people who reported feeling anxious or depressed more than half the time in the past two weeks dropped to 18 and 19 percent, respectively. In a survey in June, 28 percent reported anxiety and 21 percent reported depression.</p></div>
