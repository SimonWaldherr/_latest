Pictured, a map showing the routes by which the Black Death spread across Europe in the 14th Century</p><h3 class="mol-factbox-title">THE CAUSE BEHIND EUROPE'S BUBONIC PLAGUES</h3><p class="mol-para-with-font">The plague, caused by the bacterium Yersinia pestis, was the cause of some of the world's deadliest pandemics, including the Justinian Plague, the Black Death, and the major epidemics that swept through China in the late 1800s.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">The disease continues to affect populations around the world today.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">The Black Death of 1348 famously killed half of the people in London within 18 months, with bodies piled five-deep in mass graves.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">When the Great Plague of 1665 hit, a fifth of people in London died, with victims shut in their homes and a red cross painted on the door with the words 'Lord have mercy upon us'.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">The pandemic spread from Europe through the 14th and 19th centuries - thought to come from fleas which fed on infected rats before biting humans and passing the bacteria to them.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">But modern experts challenge the dominant view that rats caused the incurable disease.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Experts point out that rats were not that common in northern Europe, which was hit equally hard by plague as the rest of Europe, and that the plague spread faster than humans might have been exposed to their fleas.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Most people would have had their own fleas and lice, when the plague arrived in Europe in 1346, because they bathed much less often.&#160;</p> <p class="adHolder mpu" id="mpu_factbox_1"><p class="mol-ads-label-container"><p class="mol-ads-label">Advertisement</p></p>
