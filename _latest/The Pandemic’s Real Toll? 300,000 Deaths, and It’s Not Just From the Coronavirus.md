<div><p class="css-158dogj evys1bk0">Although the pandemic has mostly killed older Americans, the greatest percentage increase in excess deaths has occurred among adults ages 25 to 44, the analysis found.</p><p></p><p class="css-158dogj evys1bk0">While the number of deaths among adults ages 45 to 64 increased by 15 percent, and by 24 percent among those ages 65 to 74, deaths increased 26.5 percent among those in their mid-20s to mid-40s, a group that includes millennials.</p><p class="css-158dogj evys1bk0">Among those in the youngest age group, under 25, deaths were 2 percent below average.</p><p class="css-158dogj evys1bk0">People of color also had large percentage increases in excess deaths, compared with previous years. Hispanics experienced a 54 percent increase, while Black people saw a 33 percent rise. Deaths were 29 percent above average for American Indians or Alaska Native people, and 37 percent above average for those of Asian descent.</p><p class="css-158dogj evys1bk0">By comparison, the figure for white Americans was 12 percent, according to the analysis.</p><p class="css-158dogj evys1bk0">The report reviewed deaths from Jan. 26 to Oct. 3 of 2020, and used modeling to compare the weekly tallies with those of corresponding weeks in 2015 through 2019.</p><p class="css-158dogj evys1bk0">The researchers estimated that 299,028 more people than expected died in the United States during that period, with 198,081 deaths attributable to Covid-19 and the rest to other causes.</p><p class="css-158dogj evys1bk0">That estimate is significantly higher than the 216,025 coronavirus deaths officially reported by the C.D.C. as of Oct. 15. (The figure now <a class="css-1g7m0tk" href="https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2020/us/coronavirus-us-cases.html" title="">is nearing 221,000</a>, according to a database maintained by The New York Times.)</p><p class="css-158dogj evys1bk0"><strong class="css-8qgvsz ebyp5n10">[</strong><strong class="css-8qgvsz ebyp5n10"><a class="css-1g7m0tk" href="http://on.fb.me/1paTQ1h" title="" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Like the Science Times page on Facebook.</a></strong><strong class="css-8qgvsz ebyp5n10"> </strong>| Sign up for the <strong class="css-8qgvsz ebyp5n10"><a class="css-1g7m0tk" href="http://nyti.ms/1MbHaRU" title="" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Science Times newsletter.</a></strong><strong class="css-8qgvsz ebyp5n10">]</strong></p><p class="css-158dogj evys1bk0">Other researchers have also found greater deaths over all during the pandemic. A study published July 1 in JAMA Internal Medicine <a class="css-1g7m0tk" href="https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamainternalmedicine/fullarticle/2767980" title="" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">reported that deaths from all causes in the United States increased by 122,000</a> from March 1 to May 30, a figure that was 28 percent higher than the deaths attributed solely to Covid-19 during that period.</p></div>
