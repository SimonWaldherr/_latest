<div><p class="mol-para-with-font">Your brain reaches its 'cognitive peak' - that is when it is most powerful - at age 35, according to a study, but it starts to decline by the time you are in your mid-40s.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Ludwig Maximilian University of Munich researchers studied thousands of chess games over the past 130 years to see if our brains improve with age.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">They found a 'hump-shaped curve' in human cognitive performance when measured over the average lifespan - increasing sharply up to age 20, peaking at 35, then declining gradually from the age of 45 onwards.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">We have also been getting smarter as a species collectively, according to the team, who discovered an increase in chess playing performance over the past 125 years.&#160;&#160;</p><img id="i-6a61e938d2f58839" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIAAAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAAALAAAAAABAAEAAAIBRAA7" height="435" width="634" alt="Your brain reaches its 'cognitive peak' and is at its most powerful at about age 35, according to a new study, but it starts to go on the decline by the time you are 45. class="mol-para-with-font">Over the lifespan, performance increased sharply until the early 20s, peaked at approximately 35 years, then declined slightly after 45 years.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">It isn't just performance over a single lifespan that the team measured, they also found a notable improvement in ability since the first studied match in 1890.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Little is known about how the age pattern in individual performance in cognitively demanding tasks changed over the past century.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">The main difficulty for measuring such life cycle performance patterns and their dynamics over time is related to the construction of a reliable measure.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">That measure has to be comparable across individuals and over time and not affected by changes in technology or other environmental factors, authors said.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">The German researchers used the dynamic patterns found in 24,000 professional games of chess by analysing individual move-by-move performance since 1890.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">'This provides a precise and comparable measurement of individual performance for the same individual at different ages over long periods of time,' the team said.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">It does so by exploiting the advantage of a strictly comparable task and a comparison with an identical performance benchmark.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">'Over the past 125 years, performance has improved, especially for individuals less
