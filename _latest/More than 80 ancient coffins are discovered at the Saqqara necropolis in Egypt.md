of Cairo in the sprawling burial ground of Saqqarah, the necropolis of the ancient Egyptian capital of Memphis, a UNESCO World Heritage site</p><h3 class="mol-factbox-title">Ancient Egyptian burial rituals&#160;&#160;</h3> <p class="mol-para-with-font">Over thousands of years, ancient Egyptians developed elaborate and complex&#160;funerary practices that they believed were necessary to enter the afterlife.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">The processes evolved over time, but many elements remained consistent through different ancient Egyptian dynasties.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">The most classic form of mummification dates back to the 18th Dynasty that spanned the&#160;the period from 1549/1550 to 1292 BC.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">First, the brain was removed. Some say this was by a metal hook inserted through the nose, while others say it was with a rod through the cranium which was used to liquefy the brain.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">The brain would have then been drained out through the nose before the skull was filled with a mixture of tree resin and fragrances.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">This stopped the decomposition of any residual brain tissue, and also suppressed the smell.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Then, the lumbar area was sliced open and the organs of the abdomen were removed. The heat was usually left.&#160;These were then heavily salted and placed in canopic jars.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">The now-empty chest cavity was then filled with a mixture of aromatics.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">All incisions into the body were then filled with a salt called natron which disinfected wounds.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">The body was then put on a bed of natron for a period of between 35 and 70 days.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">At the end of this period, the now dehydrated corpse was washed, oiled and covered with resin as adhesive.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">The body was then wrapped in linen until it was fully covered.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Depending on the social status of the deceased, the mummy was sometimes then adorned with decorations, such as a mask or a shroud.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">The mummy was then buried in a tomb, ready for the afterlife.&#160;&#160;</p><p class="adHolder mpu" id="mpu_factbox_1"><p class="mol-ads-label-container"><p class="mol-ads-label">Advertisement</p></p>

EGYPTIANS EMBALMED THEIR DEAD</h3> <p class="mol-para-with-font"></p><p class="mol-para-with-font">It is thought a range of chemicals were used to embalm and preserve the bodies of the dead in ancient cultures.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Russian scientists believe&#160;a different balm was used to preserve hair fashions of the time than the concoctions deployed on the rest of the body.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Hair was treated with a balm made of a combination of beef fat, castor oil, beeswax and pine gum and with a drop of aromatic pistachio oil as an optional extra.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Mummification in ancient Egypt involved removing the corpse&#8217;s internal organs, desiccating the body with a mixture of salts, and then wrapping it in cloth soaked in a balm of plant extracts, oils, and resins.&#160;&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Older mummies are believed to have been naturally preserved by burying them in dry desert sand and were not chemically treated.&#160;&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Gas chromatography/mass spectrometry (GC/MS) techniques have been deployed in recent years in find out more about the ancient embalming process.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Studies have found bodies were embalmed with:&#160;a plant oil, such as sesame oil; phenolic acids, probably from an aromatic plant extract; and polysaccharide sugars from plants.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">The recipe also featured dehydroabietic acid and other diterpenoids from conifer resin.</p><p></p> <p class="adHolder mpu"
