They expected these business interruption policies to cover them for losses when they were forced to close during lockdown" class="blkBorder img-share">  <p class="imageCaption">Hordes of companies took out 'Resilience' policies from RSA. They expected these business interruption policies to cover them for losses when they were forced to close during lockdown</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">But RSA, along with other insurers who offered similar policies, refused to pay out &#8211; claiming that the policies were not designed to cover a nationwide pandemic.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">The Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) took RSA and seven other insurers to court, to have judges decide on the matter.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Last month, the two judges ruled that RSA should be forced to pay businesses which claimed under the Resilience policy.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">RSA was planning to appeal to the Supreme Court, the highest court in the land.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">But it has dropped its challenge, and will start assessing the value of claims and making interim payments where necessary.</p><p class="money item html_snippet module">  </p> <p class="mol-para-with-font">Sonia Campbell, a lawyer at Mishcon de Reya who is representing policyholders, said it is 'a huge win' for the businesses, but added: 'Given the insurers' approach to policyholders' claims to date, businesses should not expect it to be plain sailing.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Every policyholder has to prove its loss, and this is where we expect insurers to continue to frustrate the process.'</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">RSA declined to comment. It is still challenging the High Court's decision on other policies.</p></div>
