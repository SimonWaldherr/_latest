<div><p class="mol-para-with-font">Young people and children are at risk of becoming a 'lost generation' because of the <a href="/news/uk-government/index.html" id="mol-98039b00-12f6-11eb-b712-07e43a0e42dc">UK Government</a>'s Covid-19 response, members of SAGE warned today.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Generation Z &#8212; those aged between seven and 24 &#8212; have largely avoided any direct consequences of <a href="/news/coronavirus/index.html" id="mol-98028990-12f6-11eb-b712-07e43a0e42dc">coronavirus</a> to their health.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">But the disruption to education, huge national debt and climbing unemployment rates will all have a 'catastrophic' impact on their futures, the experts warned.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">More than 10million British schoolchildren had their schools shut in March to flatten coronavirus's curve. The move was successful but it is feared to have exacerbated inequalities and left children from the poorest families at least a year behind their classmates.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Numerous studies and reports have also warned of a 'ticking time bomb' of mental and physical health problems in the future - including anxiety, depression and obesity - as a result of youngsters being cooped up inside for the better part of six months.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">And there are fears that problems pre-dating Covid-19 could be made worse. <p class="mol-para-with-font">Professor Viner's words were echoed by another SAGE member, Professor Chris Bonell from the London School of Hygiene and Tropical Medicine.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">He said even those who got through lockdown without their mental or physical health taking a hit would be impacted by the financial fallout of the crisis, describing it as 'the most dramatic disruption' 'any generation has experienced in modern times'.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">He told the newspaper:&#160;'These adversities will be played out along their whole life courses: scarring like this doesn't just wash out &#8211; it's permanent.'&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Professor Bonell said the Government failed to think about the impact on generation Z when making its policy decisions.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">He said experts advising ministers had been voicing their concerns about the threat of some lockdown measures on young people, but that they fell on deaf ears.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Professor Bonell claimed Number 10 was shown 'empirical evidence' of the increased risk to youngsters but it was 'brushed aside'.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">One example was calls for ministers to issue a full national programme of learning instead of leaving it up to individual schools during the first wave.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">It comes after the Resolution Foundation predicted youth unemployment could top 1.3million by next year, more than trebling from the current level of 408,000.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">It estimated that around 800,000 people aged between 18 and 24 are set to leave education this year.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">With unemployment expected to soar this year as struggling firms slash costs, the think tank warns the &#8216;corona class of 2020&#8217; could face years of reduced pay and limited job prospects.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">The Resolution Foundation said evidence from previous recessions shows young people who have just left full-time education are hit harder than other age groups.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">It also pointed out that those who have recently left education are more likely to work in sectors which have been hardest hit by the lock-down.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Over the past decade, it said one-in-three non-graduates, and one-in-five graduates, have got their first employment experience after education in sectors such as retail, hospitality, travel and leisure.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Meanwhile,&#160;University of Cambridge researchers have warned&#160;lockdown could spark countless mental health problems later in life for teenagers.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Face-to-face social interaction is vital for brain development and building a sense of self between the age of 10 and 24.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font"> Cambridge academics warn depriving young people of this may lead to a host of mental health, behavioural and cognitive problems later in life.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">In an editorial in the Lancet in July, neuroscientists from the prestigious university said adolescence &#8211; defined by the scientists as between 10 and 24 &#8211; is a vulnerable stage in the development of a person.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">On top of major hormonal changes and puberty, this is the point at which people want to spend more time with their friends than their family.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">It is also the period in their life when they are most likely to develop mental health problems.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Earlier studies have suggested that high quality relationships with appear to protect people from mental health problems and strengthen their resilience.&#160;</p></div>
