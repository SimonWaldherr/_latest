<div><p>Jon Gray still remembers what it was like when he was hired as an entry-level analyst at a seven-year-old private-equity shop in New York City in 1992.</p>

<p>"It was a tiny place ... I think there were 80 or 90 people," Gray said of Blackstone, a firm that would go on to become the world's largest alternative investment manager and, during Gray's time as head of global real estate,&#160;<a href="https://www.businessinsider.com/blackstone-is-largest-owner-of-real-estate-2015-11?r=blackstone-teaser" target="_blank" rel="noopener">its largest property owner</a>.</p><p>Fresh out of the University of Pennsylvania, Gray was interviewed by the Blackstone cofounders Stephen Schwarzman and Pete Peterson themselves. He couldn't have predicted that he would eventually be named Blackstone's president and chief operating officer in 2018, becoming one of the most powerful executives on Wall Street.</p><p>"For me, a kid from suburban Chicago, I was like, 'Oh my gosh, this seems really exciting.' And it was obviously terrifying being interviewed," he recalled. "And by the way, starting was terrifying. I remember being so nervous having my first job here."</p><p><strong>Read more:</strong> <a href="https://www.businessinsider.com/how-private-equity-firms-are-hiring-data-scientists-and-analysts-2020-2">Private equity is finally warming up to data-science hiring.
