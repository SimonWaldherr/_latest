<div><p>As January beckons and the festive season draws to a close, children across the land will soon be removing the angel atop their tree, and parcelling up the <a href="https://www.telegraph.co.uk/christmas/0/best-christmas-tree-decorations-baubles-lights-tree-toppers/">decorations</a> for next year. One by one, our much-beloved Christmas trees will be left out in the cold to await their fate. This sorry sight of branches protruding from wheelie bins and limp, lacklustre trees on pavements is enough to bring a tear to one&#8217;s eye.</p>
<p>Around six million <a href="https://www.telegraph.co.uk/christmas/0/when-buy-christmas-tree-real-fake-fir-artificial-spruce-where/">Christmas trees</a> are sold in Britain each year, and the Local Government Association estimates that the cost of landfilling those trees would be approximately &#163;13.9 million. Thankfully, there are plenty of environmentally- and taxpayer-friendly ways to <a href="https://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/earth/businessandecology/recycling/11313673/Why-its-time-you-threw-out-the-Christmas-tree.html">dispose of your tree</a>.</p>
<h3>Recycle</h3>
<p>Thanks to local authorities, garden centres and community groups, <a href="https://www.telegraph.co.uk/gardening/gardeningadvice/11259120/Christmas-trees-are-they-really-sustainable.html">recycling your Christmas tree</a> couldn&#8217;t be easier. All you have to do is <a href="https://www.telegraph.co.uk/christmas/0/twelfth-night-when-should-take-christmas-decorations-epiphany-traditions/">take down your decorations</a>, remove the tree from its pot or stand, and leave it on the boundary of your plot. Alternatively, you can drop your tree at a designated communal collection point. These trees are then shredded into chippings which are used locally in parks or woodland areas.</p>
