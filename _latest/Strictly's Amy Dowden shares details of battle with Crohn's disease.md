However, research suggests a combination of factors may be responsible. These include:</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">genetics &#8211; genes you inherit from your parents may increase your risk of developing Crohn's disease</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">the immune system &#8211; the inflammation may be caused by a problem with the immune system that causes it to attack healthy bacteria in the gut</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">previous infection &#8211; a previous infection may trigger an abnormal response from the immune system</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">smoking &#8211; smokers with Crohn's disease usually have more severe symptoms than non-smokers</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">environmental factors &#8211; Crohn's disease is most common in westernised countries such as the UK, and least common in poorer parts of the world such as Africa, which suggests the environment has a part to play&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Source: NHS&#160;</p> <p class="adHolder mpu" id="mpu_factbox_1"><p class="mol-ads-label-container"><p class="mol-ads-label">Advertisement</p></p>
