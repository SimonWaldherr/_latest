<div><p>I always think I&#8217;m happier in my discreet <a href="https://www.telegraph.co.uk/fashion/style/striped-shirt-new-zoom-uniform/">uniform</a> of classic navy and olive green but, a couple of years ago, a snazzy royal blue and oatmeal herringbone tweed jacket jumped off the sale rail and into my hands. In writing, that does not really sound like such a flamboyant purchase but, for a woman with a fear of bright patterns and bold colours, it felt quite extreme at the time.</p>
<p>Afflicted by buyer&#8217;s remorse, I didn&#8217;t give my outstanding blazer much of an airing to begin with, but over time it has become one of my favourite autumn garments. Snazzy tweed perks up an old pair of jeans, adds pizzazz to cargo pants and lasts for years. Not to mention the positive effect it has on the wearer &#8211; witness the mood-boosting properties of this glorious tweed trouser suit. If a picture paints a thousand words, and all that&#8230;</p>
<p>&#8220;At this time of the year, when we have less exposure to sunlight, we can experience a drop in the levels of the neurotransmitter serotonin, and the hormone melatonin, both of which affect mood,&#8221; explains Prof Carolyn Mair, a behavioural psychologist, author of The Psychology of Fashion and founder of the website <a href="http://psychology.fashion">psychology.fashion</a>. &#8220;When we need a mood boost, a <a href="https://www.telegraph.co.uk/fashion/style/fashion-makes-us-happy-proven-mental-health-benefits-joy-bringing/">pop of colour</a> in our clothing can make all the difference.&#8221; Proof that a colourful coat can be a real pick-me-up.</p>
