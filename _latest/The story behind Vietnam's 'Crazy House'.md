<div>(CNN) &#8212; <span><a></a><p>Wild, weird, wonderful... there are many ways to describe the Crazy House (also known as Hang Nga Guesthouse) in Dalat, a relaxed mountain resort town in southern <a href="https://edition.cnn.com/travel/destinations/vietnam" target="_blank">Vietnam</a> </p></span><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>One thing's for sure: This "crazy" complex is one of a kind.</p></p><p id="ad_rect_atf_02" class="Ad__tag Article__adBody Ad__hasLabel"></p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>Standing in stark contrast to Dalat's French colonial villas, the bizarre avant garde guesthouse is a maze of spiral staircases, sculptural bedrooms, undulating surfaces, swirls of bright colors, narrow bridges and hidden nooks.</p></p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>The artist behind the structure, 79-year-old Dang Viet Nga, says it's the ultimate expression of her imagination. </p></p><span><a></a><p>"Crazy House is a culmination of my life and creativity -- it all came together in this structure," Dang, daughter of Vietnam's former general secretary Truong Chinh, tells <a href="https://www.cnn.com/travel" target="_blank">CNN Travel</a> </p></span><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>"I wanted to create something original, pioneering -- different from anything else in the world."</p></p><p id="ad_nat_btf_01" class="Ad__tag"></p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p><h3>An exercise in creativity </h3></p></p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>After earning a PhD in architecture in Moscow, Dang worked for several years in Russia then moved to Hanoi, where she worked on government projects.</p></p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>On a business trip to Dalat, Dang says she fell in love with the lush landscape, cooler climate and kind people and hoped to eventually move there.</p></p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>In 1983, she relocated to Dalat with her then 8-year-old son, Nguyen Viet Thang.</p></p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>After years of working on state-owned developments, which afforded little creativity, she felt compelled to unleash her imagination. </p></p><a href="/travel/article/crazy-house-dalat-vietnam/index.html?gallery=3"><img class="Image__image" src="https://dynaimage.cdn.cnn.com/cnn/e_blur:500,q_auto:low,w_50,c_fill,g_auto,h_28,ar_16:9/http%3A%2F%2Fcdn.cnn.com%2Fcnnnext%2Fdam%2Fassets%2F190730170521--tom8995.jpg" alt="The Crazy House has 10 guest rooms, each named after an animal or plant. "><p class="CaptionedImage__ctaText"></p></a><p class="CaptionedImage__caption">The Crazy House has 10 guest rooms, each named after an animal or plant. </p><p class="CaptionedImage__credit">Courtesy Crazy House</p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>In February 1990, she drew up plans for Crazy House. But instead of blueprints, she created a series of paintings to communicate her fantastical vision.</p></p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>As a form of expressionist architecture, the house has no right angles but rather organic forms that are designed to mirror natural elements, like mushrooms, shells, caves and spiderwebs. </p></p><p class="PullQuote__author">Dang Viet Nga, artist</p><p>&#8220;Crazy House will never really be finished. It's like a living thing. It is always changing.&#8221;</p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>"With this form, you have to try to free your mind," she says. "There are no rules -- aside from basic structural principles [to make sure it's safe and stable] -- it's all about self-expression."</p></p><p id="ad_rect_btf_01" class="Ad__tag Article__adBody Ad__hasLabel"></p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>When it came time to construct the house, Dang used various types of materials, including steel, wood and concrete. </p></p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>"My mother used a lot of concrete because it's inexpensive and very easy to play with," says her son Nguyen, who is now 44. </p></p><p class="RelatedArticle__label">Related content</p><a class="RelatedArticle__link" href="/travel/article/lonely-planet-asia-pacific-best-destinations-2019/index.html" target="_blank">Lonely Planet's best places to visit in Asia Pacific for 2019 </a><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>"Since she can create whatever shape she wants, it reflects what she is envisioning in her mind." </p></p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>Less than a year later, the guesthouse was open for business and welcoming its first guests. </p></p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p><h3>Closer to nature </h3></p></p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>Today, Crazy House feels like woodland fantasy brought to life.</p></p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>"I felt that, over the last century, people have really destroyed nature. Not just in Vietnam, but around the world," says Dang. "So I wanted to create a structure that brings people closer to nature." </p></p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>An elevated main house, which looks like it belongs in Hansel and Gretel, sits in the center of an open courtyard, surrounded by four enormous tree houses. </p></p><a href="/travel/article/crazy-house-dalat-vietnam/index.html?gallery=5"><img class="Image__image" src="https://dynaimage.cdn.cnn.com/cnn/e_blur:500,q_auto:low,w_50,c_fill,g_auto,h_28,ar_16:9/http%3A%2F%2Fcdn.cnn.com%2Fcnnnext%2Fdam%2Fassets%2F190730170748--tom9019.jpg" alt="The Crazy House has been welcoming guests since 1991. "><p class="CaptionedImage__ctaText"></p></a><p class="CaptionedImage__caption">The Crazy House has been welcoming guests since 1991. </p><p class="CaptionedImage__credit">Courtesy Crazy House</p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>Sinuous cement "branches," which double as bridges, twist and wind between the various houses so people can move from one to the next. </p></p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>At first glance, the surreal structures recall mind-bending scenes from a Salvador Dali painting or perhaps even the organic works of modernist Catalan architect Antoni Gaudi. </p></p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>The playful style continues within the tree houses, where 10 guest rooms -- each named after an animal or type of plant -- are full of organic shapes, cavern-like beds, and wooden seating areas.  </p></p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>"Reconnecting nature is one message that I want to communicate with the house," says Dang. </p></p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>"But it's also a message to others to think outside of the box. Don't limit yourself by the rules and the expectations -- free your mind and let your imagination run wild."</p></p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>Following her own advice, Dang is continues to dream up new additions to the property while Nguyen now manages the business.</p></p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>She is currently dreaming up two new gardens -- a Land Garden and a Sky Garden -- to add more greenery and flowers, as well as another tree house. </p></p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>"I would say that Crazy House will never really be finished," says Nguyen. "It's like a living thing. It is always changing."</p></p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p><h3>How to visit</h3></p></p><p class="Paragraph__component"><p>In addition to staying in one of Crazy House's guest rooms, visitors can also tour the Dalat attraction for a fee. Though there's no restaurant, visitors can purchase drinks and snacks at a stall in the garden.   </p></p><span><a></a><span><a href="https://crazyhouse.vn/" target="_blank">Crazy House</a> , S&#7889; 3 &#272;&#432;&#7901;ng Hu&#7923;nh Th&#250;c Kh&#225;ng, Ph&#432;&#7901;ng 4, Th&#224;nh ph&#7889; &#272;&#224; L&#7841;t, L&#226;m &#272;&#7891;ng, Vietnam; +84 263 3822 070</span></span></div>
