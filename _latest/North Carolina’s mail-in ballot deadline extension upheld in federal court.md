<div><p class="font--body font-copy gray-darkest ma-0 pb-md ">&#8220;North Carolina voters deserve clarity on whether they must rely on an overburdened Post Office to deliver their ballots within three days after Election Day,&#8221; Judge James A. Wynn Jr. wrote for the majority <a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/context/court-upholds-north-carolina-s-mail-in-ballot-deadline-extension/c1dbed6f-d4f5-490f-b4eb-cd2be579b917/?itid=lk_inline_manual_4" title="www.washingtonpost.com">in the opinion</a> published late Tuesday. &#8220;The need for clarity has become even more urgent in the last week, as in-person early voting started in North Carolina on October 15.&#8221;</p><p class="absolute z-0">AD</p><p class=""></p><p class="absolute z-0">AD</p><p class=""></p><p class="font--body font-copy gray-darkest ma-0 pb-md ">The fast-moving case is one of a<a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/politics/south-carolina-ballot-supreme-court/2020/10/01/874dd598-040d-11eb-b7ed-141dd88560ea_story.html?itid=lk_inline_manual_7"> series of legal battles</a> between Democrats and Republicans over the timing and mechanics of voting in the middle of the <a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/coronavirus/?itid=lk_inline_manual_7">coronavirus </a>pandemic and <a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/politics/2020/10/20/poll-north-carolina-presidential-race/?arc404=true&amp;itid=lk_inline_manual_7">ahead of a hotly contested presidential election</a>.</p><p class="font--body font-copy gray-darkest ma-0 pb-md ">The dissenting judges warned that the new deadline would &#8220;cause yet further intolerable chaos&#8221; and implored the Supreme Court to take up the North Carolina case &#8220;immediately. Not tomorrow. Not the next day. Now.&#8221;</p><p class="font--body font-copy gray-darkest ma-0 pb-md ">The Supreme Court on Monday night allowed Pennsylvania election officials to count mail-ballots received up to three days after the election and refused a Republican request to stop the procedure.</p><p class="font--body font-copy gray-darkest ma-0 pb-md ">Millions of Americans are casting ballots by mail for the first time this year because of concerns about the risks of in-person voting associated with covid-19. In North Carolina, the state board of elections voted unanimously in September to extend the deadline to receive mail-in ballots to Nov. 12 in response to concerns from the North Carolina Alliance for Retired Americans.</p><p class="absolute z-0">AD</p><p class=""></p><p class="absolute z-0">AD</p><p class=""></p><p class="font--body font-copy gray-darkest ma-0 pb-md ">North Carolina is a critical battleground state, with polls showing President Trump and former vice president Joe Biden running in a dead heat. The president won North Carolina in 2016, and along with Florida, it is seen as crucial to his reelection efforts.</p><p class="font--body font-copy gray-darkest ma-0 pb-md ">Republican legislative leaders tried to block the extension in state and federal court. A District Court judge rejected their request for an injunction because of concerns about changing voting rules so close to an election. The lawmakers then asked the appeals court to intervene.</p><p class="font--body font-copy gray-darkest ma-0 pb-md ">In its ruling, the majority said the courts must defer to state election officials to regulate their own voting procedures.</p><p class="absolute z-0">AD</p><p class=""></p><p class="font--body font-copy gray-darkest ma-0 pb-md ">&#8220;Recent actions of the Supreme Court make clear that it is up to a state to decide what election procedures are in effect on Election Day, and not federal courts,&#8221; Judge Diana Motz wrote in a separate concurring opinion.</p><p class="absolute z-0">AD</p><p class=""></p><p class="font--body font-copy gray-darkest ma-0 pb-md ">She called the arguments from House Speaker Timothy K. Moore and Senate President Philip E. Berger &#8220;deeply troubling&#8221; because they challenge measures that &#8220;remove burdens on other citizens exercising their right to vote.&#8221;</p><p class="font--body font-copy gray-darkest ma-0 pb-md ">The court said the new rules make it easier for people to vote by mail and ensure their ballots are received if there are delays &#8220;precipitated by an avalanche of mail-in ballots.&#8221;</p><p class="font--body font-copy gray-darkest ma-0 pb-md ">&#8220;This change, of course, may have its own important consequences for the health of our citizenry&#8212;in terms of unnecessary infections avoided&#8212;and our democracy&#8212;in terms of lawful ballots cast and counted,&#8221; wrote Wynn, who was joined by 12 other judges including all three on the bench nominated  by Trump.</p><p class="absolute z-0">AD</p><p class=""></p><p class="font--body font-copy gray-darkest ma-0 pb-md ">Even as it upheld the new deadline and said Republican leaders do not have legal grounds to challenge it, the court acknowledged the issues raised in the case about the scope of the election board&#8217;s authority are far from settled.</p><p class="absolute z-0">AD</p><p class=""></p><p class="font--body font-copy gray-darkest ma-0 pb-md ">The three dissenting judges said state election officials were &#8220;changing the rules in the middle of an election&#8221; and undermining the authority of North Carolina&#8217;s elected state lawmakers.</p><p class="font--body font-copy gray-darkest ma-0 pb-md ">&#8220;It takes no special genius to know what this insidious formula is producing,&#8221; wrote Judge J. Harvie Wilkinson III, who was joined by Judges G. Steven Agee and Paul V. Niemeyer. &#8220;Our country is now plagued by a proliferation of pre-election litigation that creates confusion and turmoil and that threatens to undermine public confidence in the federal courts, state agencies, and the elections themselves.&#8221;</p><p class="font--body font-copy gray-darkest ma-0 pb-md bold">
