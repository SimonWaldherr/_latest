<div><p class="css-38z03z">Exit polls have suggested that Evo Morales&#8217; leftwing party has pulled off a stunning political comeback in Bolivia&#8217;s presidential election, although an official result has yet to emerge.</p><p class="css-38z03z">Two private surveys projected that Luis Arce, the candidate for Morales&#8217; Movimiento al Socialismo (Mas), had secured more than 50% of the vote in the ballot on Sunday, with his closest rival, the centrist former president Carlos Mesa, receiving about 30%.</p><p id="sign-in-gate"></p><p class="css-38z03z">Arce, a former finance minister under Morales, claimed victory in a late night broadcast from La Paz. &#8220;We have reclaimed democracy and above all we have reclaimed hope,&#8221; said the 57-year-old UK-educated economist, widely known as Lucho.</p><p class="css-38z03z">Arce vowed to end the uncertainty that has plagued his bitterly divided nation since October 2019, when hotly disputed claims of vote rigging against his party resulted in mass street protests, the presidential election being scrapped and Morales being forced from the country by security forces in what his supporters call a racist, rightwing coup.</p><p class="css-38z03z">&#8220;We will govern for all Bolivians ... we will bring unity to our country,&#8221; said Arce, who holds a master&#8217;s degree from the University of Warwick.</p><p class="css-38z03z">Morales, who has towered over the election re-run despite living in exile in Argentina, <a href="https://twitter.com/evoespueblo/status/1318066318453637121?s=20">hailed</a> &#8220;a resounding victory&#8221; for his party. &#8220;Sisters and brothers: the will of the people has prevailed,&#8221; tweeted Bolivia&#8217;s first indigenous president, a key member of Latin America&#8217;s leftwing <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2007/mar/16/thepinktideflows">pink tide</a> who governed from 2006 until his dramatic downfall last year.</p>             <img alt="Evo Morales speaks from exile in Buenos Aires, Argentina" src="https://i.guim.co.uk/img/media/50d3b72f13861ca7ce6c7da59786d4a28393c1e7/0_210_4469_2683/master/4469.jpg?width=445&amp;quality=85&amp;auto=format&amp;fit=max&amp;s=fdbb0ee0a14ac4accbb5a01018d4b4da" height="2683" width="4469"><p class="css-1amgqzt"></p><p class="css-19x4pdv">Evo Morales speaks from exile in Buenos Aires, Argentina, on Sunday.</p> Photograph: Juan Ignacio Roncoroni/EPA<p class="css-38z03z">Even Morales&#8217; nemesis, the rightwing interim president, Jeanine &#193;&#241;ez, conceded the left had come out on top. &#8220;We do not yet have the official count, but the data we do have shows that Mr Arce [has] &#8230; won the election. I congratulate the winners and ask them to govern thinking of Bolivia and of democracy,&#8221; &#193;&#241;ez <a href="https://twitter.com/JeanineAnez/status/1318048552191483904?s=20">tweeted</a>.</p><p class="css-38z03z">Leading members of the Latin American left, who hope Arce&#8217;s apparent triumph might help revive <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2016/may/26/south-america-progressive-dream">their fortunes</a>, celebrated the result.</p><p class="css-38z03z">&#8220;Viva the Bolivian people! Viva democracy!&#8221; <a href="https://twitter.com/gleisi/status/1318043451078881281?s=20">tweeted</a> Gleisi Hoffmann, the president of the Brazilian Workers&#8217; party (PT).</p><p class="css-38z03z">Venezuela&#8217;s authoritarian leader, Nicol&#225;s Maduro, <a href="https://twitter.com/NicolasMaduro/status/1318051486325157888?s=20">tweeted</a>: &#8220;A great victory! United and aware, the Bolivian people have used votes to defeat the coup they carried out against our brother Evo.&#8221;</p><p class="css-38z03z">If confirmed, the victory would represent a sensational political fightback for Mas, which was left reeling last year when its leader was forced to flee the country after trying to secure an unprecedented fourth term as president.</p><p class="css-38z03z">One exit poll suggested Arce had achieved <a href="https://eldeber.com.bo/usted-elige/arce-gana-en-cinco-departamentos-mesa-en-tres-y-camacho-en-uno_205199">a thumping victory</a>, winning a majority in five of Bolivia&#8217;s nine departments. The poll said Arce had secured more than 65% of the vote in La Paz, 63% in Cochabamba, 62% in Oruro and 51% in Potos&#237;.</p><p class="css-38z03z">It may be several days before the official result is confirmed. By Monday morning electoral authorities said that with about 15% of votes counted 34% had gone to Arce and nearly 44% to Mesa.</p></div>
