<div><p class="teal-video-wrap"></p><p class="gnt_em_vt"></p><p class="gnt_em_vb gnt_em_vb__te"></p><p class="gnt_ar_b_p">The&#160;largest wildfire in Colorado history continued to rage on Wednesday, and a red flag warning through Thursday morning was expected to complicate firefighters' battle against the Cameron Peak Fire.</p><p class="gnt_ar_b_p">The forecast called for winds of nearly 60 mph Wednesday night&#160;over the western portions of the fire, according to <a href="https://inciweb.nwcg.gov/incident/6964/" target="_blank" rel="noopener" class="gnt_ar_b_a">fire officials</a>, before cold&#160;and windy weather with the possibility of snow moves into the area later Thursday into early Friday.</p><p class="gnt_ar_b_p">"We are looking at another&#160;critical fire weather day like last Wednesday and Friday,'' Paul Delmerico, the fire's operations section chief,&#160;said during a video update. "These conditions are really tough for firefighters and if the fire picks up, we will move those folks into safe places.''</p><p class="gnt_ar_b_p">The Cameron Peak Fire&#160;<a href="https://www.coloradoan.com/story/news/2020/10/20/colorado-cameron-peak-fire-spawns-rare-smokenado/5993888002/" target="_blank" rel="noopener" class="gnt_ar_b_a">grew by nearly 70,000 acres</a> Friday through Sunday as firefighters faced wind gusts of more than 70 mph and a rare smokenado that was photographed by at least one local resident. By Wednesday night, the fire had grown to more than 206,000 acres.&#160;</p><img class="gnt_em_img_i" alt="Lorri Provow of Masonville took this photo of a smokenado behind the Masonville Post Office on Oct. 17, 2020."><p class="gnt_em_img_ccw gnt_em_img_ccw__cap gnt_em_img_ccw__crd"></p><p class="gnt_ar_b_p">The fire began Aug. 13 on the Arapaho and Roosevelt National Forests near Cameron Pass and Chambers Lake. It was 55% contained as of Wednesday night.&#160;The cause is still unknown.</p><p class="gnt_ar_b_p">Colorado Fire Prevention &amp; Control tweeted a video on Sunday showing firefighters&#160;bracing against wind with flames in the background.&#160;</p><p class="gnt_ar_b_p">A photo taken by Masonville resident&#160;Lorri Provow showed a smokenado in the air, which she said lasted only for a few minutes.</p><p class="gnt_ar_b_p">A smokenado is similar to a smoke tornado, <a href="https://www.usatoday.com/story/news/weather/2020/08/16/fire-tornado-loyalton-fire-california-warning/5597082002/" target="_blank" rel="noopener" class="gnt_ar_b_a">firenado</a>, fire whirl or fire devil.&#160;</p><p class="gnt_ar_b_p">Greg Hansen, a meteorologist for the National Weather Service in Boulder, Colorado, said the condition is caused when ash and smoke are quickly uplifted by an intense updraft created over hot&#160;spots of a fire.&#160;</p><p class="gnt_ar_b_p">"They are similar to a land spout tornado&#160;where existing wind shear causes an updraft with the building of a thunderstorm,'' he told the Fort Collins Coloradoan, part of the USA TODAY Network. "In this case, the rising comes from the&#160;heat of the fire.''</p><p class="gnt_ar_b_p">The nearby Calwood Fire, which&#160;forced thousands of evacuations after sparking on Saturday and spreading quickly through Boulder County, was nearing 10,000 acres on Wednesday night. Containment was at 24%.</p><p class="gnt_ar_b_p">Contributing:&#160;Miles Blumhardt, Fort Collins (Colo.)
