Amnesty International said it had obtained credible reports of deaths. </p><p>The army denies the reports. Officials have promised an investigation.</p><p>An indefinite 24-hour curfew has been imposed on Lagos and other regions. </p><p>The BBC's Nduka Orjinmo in Nigeria says a small group of protesters were defying the curfew on Wednesday and had gathered at the Lekki toll plaza in Lagos where the shooting took place.</p><p>Protests over a now-disbanded police unit, the Special Anti-Robbery Squad (Sars), have been continuing for two weeks.</p><p>Reacting to Tuesday's shootings in the wealthy Lekki suburb, <a href="https://twitter.com/HillaryClinton/status/1318676338354606081" class="css-yidnqd-InlineLink e1no5rhv0">former US Secretary of State Hillary Clinton called on Nigerian President Muhammadu Buhari and the army "to stop killing young #EndSARS protesters".</a></p><p>Nigerian footballer Odion Jude Ighalo, who plays for Manchester United, accused the Nigerian government of killing its own citizens. <a href="https://twitter.com/ighalojude/status/1318666220447715329?s=19" class="css-yidnqd-InlineLink e1no5rhv0">"I'm ashamed of this government," he said in a video posted on Twitter.</a></p><p class="css-72p885-ComponentWrapper-CrossheadComponentWrapper e1xue1i81"><h2 class="css-1jlqpzd-StyledHeading e1fj1fc10">What do we know about the shooting?</h2></p><p>Eyewitnesses spoke of uniformed men opening fire at the Lekki toll gate on Tuesday evening.</p><p>Armed soldiers were seen barricading the protest site moments before the shooting, BBC Nigeria correspondent Mayeni Jones reports. </p><p>Social media footage streamed live from the scene shows protesters tending to the wounded. </p><p>An unnamed witness told BBC News that shortly before 19:00 local time soldiers "pulled up... and they started firing directly" at peaceful protesters. </p><p>"They were firing and they were advancing straight at us. It was chaos. Somebody got hit straight beside me and he died on the spot. </p><p>"It was pandemonium and they kept on shooting and shooting at us. It lasted for about an hour-and-a-half and the soldiers were actually taking up the dead bodies."</p><p>He said the soldiers had built a barricade and ambulances could not reach the protest area.</p><p>Four witnesses told Reuters news agency soldiers had opened fire on demonstrators. One of them,  Alfred Ononugbo, 55, said: "They started firing ammunition toward the crowd. They were firing into the crowd. I saw the bullet hit one or two persons."</p><p>Authorities have only confirmed that some people were wounded in the shooting. <a working on verifying how many."</p><p class="css-72p885-ComponentWrapper-CrossheadComponentWrapper e1xue1i81"><h2 class="css-1jlqpzd-StyledHeading e1fj1fc10">How have the authorities innocent citizens."</a></p><p>He said the state authorities had ordered an "investigation into the incident". </p><p>The army has not issued a statement on events in Lekki, but in several posts on class="css-72p885-ComponentWrapper-CrossheadComponentWrapper e1xue1i81"><h2 class="css-1jlqpzd-StyledHeading e1fj1fc10">Nigeria reacts to harrowing scenes</h2></p><p><b class="css-14iz86j-BoldText e5tfeyi0">Analysis by Mayeni Jones, BBC Nigeria Correspondent</b></p><p>It was a bleak night in Nigeria as social media footage from the shooting poured in, showing gunshots ringing out at the protest site long into the night. </p><p>This is not the first time the Nigerian army has been accused of shooting unarmed protesters. There have been reports of violent crackdowns on EndSars protesters in other parts of the country. </p><p>But seeing live rounds used at one of the protest sites that had been peaceful until last night has rattled many. Just last week I stood at the very site of the shooting. The protesters were peaceful, organised, hopeful for the future of their country. But this is no more. Harrowing social media videos showing protesters singing the national anthem as shots ring out in the background have caused outrage. </p><p>Multiple online accounts say the CCTV and lights were taken out at a toll gate where the protest took place before troops started advancing, leading to total chaos. These details are galvanising a generation already disenfranchised with the ruling class. The deafening silence from the presidency is only exacerbating this anger. The Nigerian government is running out of time to quell the growing dissatisfaction.</p><span class="css-18syo2x-Placeholder e16icw910"><span><img alt="2px presentational grey line" src="https://c.files.bbci.co.uk/1226D/production/_105894347_grey_line-nc.png" width="640" height="2" class="css-evoj7m-Image ee0ct7c0"></span></span><p class="css-72p885-ComponentWrapper-CrossheadComponentWrapper e1xue1i81"><h2 class="css-1jlqpzd-StyledHeading e1fj1fc10">How did the unrest begin?</h2></p><p>Protests began nearly two weeks ago with calls for the Sars, which had been accused of illegal detentions, assaults and shootings, to be disbanded.</p><p>President Buhari dissolved the unit on 11 October.</p><p class="css-bhez6r-Container e1h75hkw0"></p><p class="css-178wc68-VisuallyHidden e1y6uwnp0">media caption</p>Who is policing Nigeria's police?<p>But the demonstrators called for more changes in the security forces as well as reforms to the way the country is run.</p><p>Lagos state Governor Babajide Sanwo-Olu has said that criminals have hijacked the protests.</p></div>
