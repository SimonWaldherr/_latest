<div><p class="mol-para-with-font">A coronavirus patient who was cheered out of intensive care just months ago after surviving a 60-day battle with the disease has died.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Aerospace engineer Roehl Ribaya, 47, was the last Covid-19 patient to leave Blackpool Victoria Hospital's ICU in July after spending nearly two months&#160;on a mechanical ventilator.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">The father-of-one was moved into a general ward for rehab after clearing the infection and was eventually discharged on August 14.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">But he suffered a cardiac arrest at home in Lytham, Lancashire, on October 13 and died in hospital two days later. His widow, nurse Stella Ricio-Ribaya, said he 'never recovered' from the long-term effects of the virus.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">The family believe Mr&#160;Ribaya's death was linked to 'Long Covid', a series of complications that linger after the original illness has cleared up.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">But no post-mortem has been held and the family are not aware if an inquest will take place. class="mol-factbox-title">WHAT IS LONG COVID AND CAN IT KILL YOU?</h3> <p class="mol-para-with-font">Most coronavirus patients will recover within a fortnight, with the tell-tale symptoms a fever, cough and lost sense of smell or taste that last for between several days and a couple of weeks.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Some people develop more serious illness such as pneumonia and can die, while others suffer effects for months after they have been infected and recovered from the initial illness.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">'Long Covid' symptoms can include:&#160;</p><ul class="mol-bullets-with-font"><li>Chronic tiredness</li><li>Breathlessness</li><li>Raised heart rate</li><li>Delusions</li><li>Strokes</li><li>Insomnia</li><li>Loss of taste/smell</li><li>Kidney disease</li><li>Mobility issues</li><li>Headaches</li><li>Muscle pains</li><li class="class">Fevers</li></ul><p class="mol-para-with-font">And growing amounts of research suggest that people may suffer severe and even life-threatening consequences of having had Covid-19.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">If the virus infects the blood vessels and the heart it can cause a condition called myocarditis, which makes the organ swell up and can lead to abnormal heartbeats and a reduced ability to pump blood normally.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">While around half of people who get this recover without complications, <a class="class" rel="nofollow noreferrer noopener" target="_blank" href="https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/covid-19-can-wreck-your-heart-even-if-you-havent-had-any-symptoms/">Scientific American</a>&#160;reported, some can suffer from permanent heart damage and an increased risk of later problems such as heart failure or even death.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">The lungs can also suffer permanent damage from coronavirus infection, with a type of scarring called fibrosis developing in the organs and making it harder to breathe or causing ongoing coughing.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Seriously reduced lung function can starve other vital organs and muscles of oxygen and damage people's overall health, forcing the heart to do more work and making it harder for the body to clear chest infections that can lead to pneumonia.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Data from the COVID Symptom Study app, by King's College London and health company Zoe, suggests one in ten people may still have symptoms after three weeks, and some may suffer for months.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">There is not yet reliable data about the numbers of people who have died as a consequence of 'long Covid' but the significant damage the the virus can do to the heart and lungs suggests it is a possibility.&#160;&#160;&#160;</p><p class="adHolder mpu" id="mpu_factbox_1"><p class="mol-ads-label-container"><p <p class="mol-para-with-font">She said: 'He was never the same. He was so breathless all the time. Please follow the government's advice so we can stop this virus. He was taken too soon. We don't want any more to die.'</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Close friend Mark Delabajan said the family were 'devastated'.&#160;He said Mr Ribaya's cause of death was cardiac arrest with the secondary cause given as post-Covid pulmonary fibrosis.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Mark added: 'It was long Covid. His breathing was never the same and he couldn't get up the stairs. He was rushed back into hospital a number of times.'</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">He revealed how his friend was the 'life and soul of the party... very funny and always joking around'.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">'The staff in Blackpool Victoria Hospital were very fond of him,' he added.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Mr Ribaya arrived at the Blackpool hospital on 29 May and spent 48 days in intensive care on a ventilator.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">In July, when he was clapped out of intensive care, lead consultant Dr Jason Cupitt said it signalled they had 'survived the first wave of this silent killer'.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Mr Cupitt said at the time: 'It's amazing to be able to wave goodbye to our last Covid patient.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">'While we have become very fond of Roehl, the staff are so happy to say farewell as it sends out the message we've survived the first wave of this silent killer.'&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Mr Cupitt admitted the 2020 pandemic is the biggest 'emotional rollercoaster' in his 19 years as a consultant.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">He added: 'One of the hardest things was having to talk to families over the phone who could not be with their loved ones.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">'There are patients I will certainly remember for the rest of my life as they were part of the most frightening and exciting time of my career.'&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Today Kevin McGee, chief executive of Blackpool Teaching Hospitals NHS Foundation Trust, said their staff were devastated at the death.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Mr McGee added: 'We were extremely saddened to hear about the death of Roehl and our thoughts and prayers are with his family at this sad time.'</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">It comes after a study on Monday found nearly two-thirds of <a href="/news/coronavirus/index.html" id="mol-f057e2a0-12ee-11eb-9127-47f9b62444ed">coronavirus</a>&#160;hospital patients are still suffering organ damage months after being discharged.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Oxford University experts found 60 per cent of patients had scarring or inflammation in their lungs three months after beating the virus.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">MRI scans showed 30 per cent had lasting damage to their kidney, 26 per cent their heart and 10 per cent their liver. The Covid survivors also showed signs of tissue changes in parts of their brain, and performed poorer in cognitive and physical tests.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Two-thirds suffered from persistent breathlessness and 55 per cent showed signs of mental and physical fatigue.&#160;&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">The study of 58 Covid-19 survivors adds to the growing concern about the health and wellbeing of people who manage to beat a bad bout of the disease.&#160;</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Most people who contract Covid-19 will recover within a fortnight, suffering a fever, cough and losing their sense of smell or taste for several days.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">The small number who are hospitalised are often either elderly or have underlying health conditions - including heart disease, diabetes and asthma.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">For this reason, the team at Oxford said it's 'difficult to know' exactly how much of the damage seen in patients' organs was 'pre-existing and how much has been caused by Covid-19'.</p></div>
