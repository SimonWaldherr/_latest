<p>With less than two weeks remaining in the presidential contest, Joe Biden's campaign enjoys a massive cash advantage over President Trump's. </p>   <p>The president's campaign committee finished September with <a href="https://docquery.fec.gov/cgi-bin/forms/C00580100/1458436/">$63.1 million</a> in its coffers, compared with the Biden team's <a href="https://docquery.fec.gov/cgi-bin/forms/C00703975/1458940/">$177.3 million</a> cash on hand, according to new filings with the Federal Election Commission late Tuesday evening.</p>   <p>Democrats were quick to <a href="https://twitter.com/rufusgifford/status/1318694924364238849">delight</a> in Trump's lagging figures, touting their cash on hand numbers in Tuesday night <a href="https://twitter.com/lmguinn/status/1318716168509808642">tweets</a>.</p>   <p>It's a severe reversal for Trump, who began his <a href="https://www.npr.org/2020/05/20/858347477/money-tracker-how-much-trump-and-biden-have-raised-in-the-2020-election">reelection campaign</a> with a large cash advantage.</p>   <p>The Biden campaign announced last week that along with allied Democratic groups, it raised a record-setting <a href="https://www.npr.org/2020/10/14/923851384/biden-announces-record-383-million-september-haul">$383 million in September alone</a> &#8212; <a href="https://www.npr.org/2020/10/16/924362863/trump-and-republicans-raise-248-million-in-september-lagging-democrats">about $135 million more</a> than the Trump campaign and Republican affiliates collected last month.</p>   <p>Last week Trump communications director Tim Murtaugh <a href="https://twitter.com/TimMurtaugh/status/1316918739024175104">tweeted </a>that the Trump campaign, the Republican National Committee and joint committees had $251 million cash on hand, writing, "President Trump hits final stretch with strength, resources, record &amp; huge ground game needed to spread message and secure re-election."</p>   
    

    <p id="ad-backstage-News_Politics_Elections" class="ad-backstage"></p>

   <p>Meanwhile, the Biden campaign <a href="https://www.npr.org/2020/10/14/923851384/biden-announces-record-383-million-september-haul">announced </a>that along with the Democratic National Committee and affiliated groups, the campaign had a whopping $432 million in the bank.</p>   <p>A candidate's own campaign committee has the most flexibility with regard to spending.</p>   <p>Trump's campaign has been <a href="https://www.npr.org/2020/10/13/923427969/presidential-campaign-tv-ad-spending-crosses-1-billion-mark-in-key-states">outspent </a>on television ads in recent weeks, as Biden has dominated the airwaves, particularly in Pennsylvania, Michigan, and Wisconsin &#8212; three critical swing states. </p>   <p>Defending himself against Biden's major fundraising advantage, Trump told a crowd in Erie, Pa., on Tuesday that he could be the "king of all fundraisers" if he wanted.</p>   <p>"I would be the greatest that ever lived," Trump said. "All they have to do is give me a list of the top hundred companies, I'll call the president of every one. They will pay me whatever I want. I would set every record. The problem is, I'd owe them."</p>
